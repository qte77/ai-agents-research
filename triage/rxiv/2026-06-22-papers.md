### Your UnEmbedding Matrix is Secretly a Feature Lens for Text Embeddings

- arxiv: <https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07502>
- summary: This paper introduces EmbedFilter, a linear transformation that refines text embeddings from large language models (LLMs) by suppressing the influence of high-frequency tokens, leading to improved zero-shot performance on downstream tasks.
- methods: linear transformation, filtering high-frequency tokens
- findings: High-frequency tokens suppress the model's ability to capture nuanced semantics.; EmbedFilter enhances semantic representations by filtering out a latent subspace.; LLMs with EmbedFilter achieve superior zero-shot performance with reduced embedding dimensions.

### Sparse Subspace-to-Expert Sharing for Task-Agnostic Continual Learning

- arxiv: <https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07500>
- summary: SETA is a framework that addresses the plasticity-stability dilemma in continual learning for Large Language Models by using adaptive sparse subspace decomposition into task-specific expert modules.
- methods: Mixture of Sparse Experts, adaptive sparse subspace decomposition, adaptive elastic anchoring, routing-aware regularization, unified gating network
- findings: SETA achieves competitive or superior overall performance relative to state-of-the-art continual learning baselines; strong retention of early-task knowledge; improved backward transfer

### How AI Agents Reshape Knowledge Work: Autonomy, Efficiency, and Scope

- arxiv: <https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07489>
- summary: The study examines how AI agents, specifically Perplexity's Computer product, enhance knowledge work by automating tasks, reducing completion time, and improving output quality compared to traditional search methods.
- methods: empirical analysis, natural experiments
- findings: Computer performs 26 minutes of autonomous work per user session, compared to 33 seconds for Search.; Computer reduces completion time from 269 to 36 minutes on matched tasks, lowering estimated time and cost by 87% and 94%, respectively.; Computer queries more often cross occupational boundaries and require higher-order cognition.
